Hydrolysis of Amides

HKDSE Chemistry Topic 11: Chemistry of Carbon Compounds

Reaction Principles

The Amide Functional Group

Amides contain the -CONH2 functional group. The Carbon-Nitrogen single bond is surprisingly strong due to partial double-bond character (resonance with the carbonyl oxygen). Therefore, hydrolysis of amides is slow and requires prolonged heating under reflux with a catalyst (dilute acid or alkali).

General Structure:

\( R-CO-NH_2 \)

1. Acidic Hydrolysis

Heating with dilute acid (e.g., \( H_2SO_4(aq) \) or \( HCl(aq) \)). The amide is converted to a carboxylic acid and an ammonium salt.

\( CH_3CONH_2 + H_2O + H^+ \xrightarrow{\Delta} CH_3COOH + NH_4^+ \)

Note: Ammonia is basic, so it reacts with the acid to form \( NH_4^+ \).

2. Alkaline Hydrolysis

Heating with dilute alkali (e.g., \( NaOH(aq) \)). The amide is converted to a carboxylate salt and ammonia gas.

\( CH_3CONH_2 + OH^- \xrightarrow{\Delta} CH_3COO^- + NH_3(g) \)

Note: The carboxylic acid formed initially is neutralized by the alkali to form a carboxylate ion.

Common Pitfalls

Student Error

"Acidic hydrolysis of an amide releases ammonia gas (\( NH_3 \))."

Correct Concept

Ammonia is a base. In acidic conditions (e.g., presence of \( H^+ \)), it immediately reacts to form the ammonium ion (\( NH_4^+ \)). No gas is observed escaping the reaction mixture.

Student Error

"Alkaline hydrolysis produces a carboxylic acid."

Correct Concept

Carboxylic acids are acidic. In the presence of an alkali (like NaOH), the acid is instantly neutralized to form a carboxylate salt (e.g., \( CH_3COO^-Na^+ \)).

Student Error

"Adding dilute acid/alkali at room temperature is enough to hydrolyze the amide."

Correct Concept

The C-N bond is very strong due to resonance stabilization. The reaction requires significant activation energy. Prolonged heating under reflux is absolutely necessary.

Quick Revision Summary

  • Reaction Type: Hydrolysis (cleavage of bonds by addition of water/reagents).
  • Condition Required: Heat under reflux (due to strong C-N bond).
  • Acidic Pathway:
    Amide + \(H_2O\) + \(H^+\) \(\rightarrow\) Carboxylic Acid + Ammonium Salt (\(NH_4^+\)).
  • Alkaline Pathway:
    Amide + \(OH^-\) \(\rightarrow\) Carboxylate Salt + Ammonia Gas (\(NH_3\)).
  • Distinguishing Test: Heating with NaOH(aq). Only amides (and ammonium salts) release \(NH_3\) gas that turns moist red litmus blue.
